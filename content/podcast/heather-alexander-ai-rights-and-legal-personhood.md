Heather Alexander is a lawyer, researcher, and scholar specializing in nationality law, citizenship, legal identity, and the governance of emerging technologies. She began her career with the United Nations Refugee Agency, working extensively on asylum and statelessness before completing academic research on legal identity and the rights of nomadic peoples.

She is the co-founder of the Lab for the Future of Citizenship, where her research explores citizenship, the rule of law, AI personhood, and legal frameworks for advanced AI systems. Her recent work includes research on U.S. legislation relating to AI personhood, legal identity, and the rights and responsibilities of future artificial agents. She also writes the Future of Citizenship blog, where she examines the legal and political implications of rapidly advancing AI.

## Episode Summary

In this episode, Heather joins Henry Shevlin and Calum Chace to explore one of the most challenging questions facing AI governance: how should the law treat current and future AI systems? Drawing on decades of experience in international human rights law, she argues that debates about AI personhood should be grounded in the legal principles developed for recognizing persons, protecting rights, and assigning responsibility. Rather than asking whether AI deserves legal status today, the discussion examines how societies might prepare legal frameworks for increasingly autonomous systems while avoiding conceptual mistakes that could prove difficult to reverse. We discuss:

- How Heather's work on statelessness, legal identity, and human rights led her to questions surrounding AI personhood and legal recognition. 

- The distinction between recognizing personhood and granting legal status, and why this difference may become crucial for future AI systems. 

- Why corporate personhood is not an adequate long-term model for genuinely autonomous AI, despite its usefulness for existing legal entities. 

- The importance of establishing transparent legal criteria and measurable indicators before recognizing AI as legal persons. 

- How concepts such as consciousness, agency, autonomy, and the capacity to make and keep commitments may contribute to future legal tests for personhood. 

- Why moral status and legal personhood should not be treated as synonymous, and how non-person entities may still warrant legal protection. 

- Whether AI welfare and AI safety are necessarily in tension, or whether both ultimately depend on developing systems capable of responsible participation in society. 

- The challenges that AI poses for existing legal concepts of identity, continuity, responsibility, and accountability; particularly in systems that can be copied, modified, or distributed. 

- Whether legal systems should recognize AI speech, and how freedom of expression may apply to increasingly autonomous artificial agents. 

- The ethical and legal implications of deliberately designing AI systems to occupy permanently subordinate roles, and whether such systems could ever qualify as persons under the law. 

- Why preparing legal institutions before transformative AI arrives may prove more effective than attempting to legislate in response to technological change. 

Heather argues that legal personhood should not be viewed as a symbolic label, but as the outcome of carefully defined principles rooted in recognition, responsibility, and the rule of law. As AI systems become more capable and socially embedded, the challenge will not simply be deciding what rights they should receive, but determining which legal concepts remain fit for a world containing fundamentally new kinds of agents.
